Workshop Series (context)

The series frames the philosophy statement alongside three companion areas: Contributions to Teaching, Teaching Service & Scholarship, and Peer Sharing. Sessions are led by Jefferson faculty across the sequence, culminating in the Educational Philosophy Statement focus.

Why “Tell Your Story” (and not slogans)?

A memorable teaching statement anchors on specific stories, strategies, and evidence—rather than generic claims. As James Lang notes, committees remember concrete narrative moments, not stock phrases about “student-centered classrooms.”

Your Resource Streams

  • Contributions to Teaching — roles, courses, innovations, assessments, mentoring.
  • Scholarship of Teaching & Learning (SoTL) — design, study, dissemination of teaching/learning improvements; follow accepted academic standards for digital/educational scholarship.
  • Personifying the Jefferson Educator — reflect across the eight guiding domains below.

Jefferson Educator – Guiding Domains

Assessment for Learning Critical Self-Reflection Evidence-based Teaching Innovation Learner-Centeredness Learning Design Learning Environment Management Professional Vitality

Use these domains to generate examples, artifacts, and claims in your statement; connect each claim to evidence (student work, outcomes data, peer observation, or scholarship).

Prompts to Draft Your Statement

  • Student perspective: If your learners described your teaching, what consistent themes would they mention?
  • Signature activity: Describe one activity that best reflects the teacher you aim to be. Why does it work? What learning evidence do you see?
  • Learning goals & evidence: What do you want students to learn? How do you know goals are met (artifacts, performance, feedback)?
  • Growth over time: How has your approach changed? What prompted the change (data, reflection, literature)?

Shape & Flow (a workable outline)

  1. Opening stance: A 2–3 sentence teaching ethos tied to a real context.
  2. Approach in practice: Brief stories that illustrate tactics aligned to your goals and learner needs.
  3. Evidence of impact: Link claims to artifacts, results, or scholarship.
  4. Ongoing growth: How you iterate—reflection, peer review, assessment cycles.

Peer Review & Rubric Cues

Use a concise rubric to review each other’s drafts: clarity of stance, alignment to evidence, coherence, and growth reflection. Incorporate fix-forward suggestions (specific next edit or artifact to add) rather than only critique.

Try It with Copilot

  • “Draft a 150-word opening that shows my stance on Assessment for Learning with one story.”
  • “Rewrite this paragraph to foreground learner evidence, not my intentions.”
  • “List three artifacts that could support my claim about inclusive small-group facilitation.”
